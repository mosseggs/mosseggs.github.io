Make it Obvious

You want to make it as obvious as possible for your user what he should do next, and you can do that with a banana.

One of the best ways to remind yourself about what's really going on is to think of a monkey in a big red fez... The best way to motivate the monkey, of course, is to use a banana. Whenever the monkey walks into a new situation, all it wants to know is, "Where's the banana?"

When [users] visit a page, they decide if they're going to stay in less [than] three seconds. If they don't know what to do, they hit the "Back" button. Hence the banana (remember the fez!). Force yourself to design each and every page with one and only one objective.

  • Make the banana the most obvious thing on the page
  • Make it easy to start, easy to keep going, easy to finish and easy to stay with.
  • The minute it's not easy, lazy people will flee. And everyone is lazy sometimes.
Seth Godin, The Big Red Fez: How to Make Any Web Site Better, p. 7, 17, 21, 77 (emphasis added)

You want users to see and understand as much as possible as quickly as possible:

  • Take advantage of conventions
  • Create effective visual hierarchies
  • Break pages up into clearly defined areas
  • Make it obvious what's clickable
  • Eliminate distractions
  • Format content to support scanning
Steve Krug, Don't Make Me Think, p. 29

Deciding on an organization

  • Users can move easily via intuitive navigation.
  • What are the goals of your site?
  • Why will people come to your site? Who are they?
  • For every “who” write a scenario of how they might experience your website-to-be (how, what, when, where, why …)
  • Target your content (prose, voice & imagery) to best serve those whos and scenarios you’ve imagined
  • Give every unit of content inventory a separate card
  • Group the cards (try at least 3 versions)
  • Get a friend to try it (is it similar?)
  • Name the groups you decide on (test)
  • This is the (initial) basis of your site structure

Organization Schemes

  • Exact: Well defined & mutually exclusive sections
    • Alphabetical (Blooberry CSS)
    • Chronological (Maya Lin)
    • Geographical (FedEx)
    • Scales (ratings, like Amazon)
  • Ambiguous: Groups items in intellectually (subjectively) meaningful ways
    • Topical (w3schools)
    • Task-oriented (functional) (USPS)
    • Audience specific (MCC)
    • Metaphor driven (myCourses Mgmt page)
    • Hybrid …

Navigational Elements

  • Integrated into page view
    • Navigation bars
    • Pull-down menus
  • Remote/supplemental pages
    • Table of contents
    • Index
    • Site map
    • Guided tour

Page design for web vision

  1. Create a clear visual hierarchy
    • Show me the priorities!
  2. Take advantage of conventions
    • Don’t make me relearn!
  3. Break pages into clearly defined areas
    • Don’t make me hunt!
  4. Make it obvious what’s clickable
    • Don’t make me think!
  5. Minimize noise (busyness & background)
    • Don’t distract me!

Putting it all together

  • Periodically draw & update site structure
    • This determines what navigational elements will be wanted (in addition to the actual content)
  • Periodically draw & update blueprints of page template(s) structure
    • First roughly represent relative importance of necessary elements (nav/functional & content)
    • Gradually evolve into layout grid, and finally fully-designed mock-ups
  • Prototype, test & iterate

and the Content?

CONTENT IS KING!
  • Page = single coherent topic
  • Make text content scannable
    • Summary first, meaningful headings & subheadings, highlight key ideas
    • Skimming title, summary & major headings alone should give a broad overview
    • Might need top-of-page links to headings
  • Provide unambiguous, spottable & relevant links
    • again, embedded vs separate?
  • Paragraph = clear, one-idea chunk from your unique perspective
  • Tighten up your text:
    • Repetition?
    • Extra adverbs/adjectives?
    • Intros & transitions?
    • $2 words used where 20¢ would suffice?
    • Negative? Passive? 
    • "Happy talk must die" -Steve Krug
  • Good Thing in general: Activate the passive!
    • Sentence subjects name the cast of characters
    • Sentence verbs name the crucial actions those characters are part of
    • The subject of the sentence should do the acting, not be acted upon.
    • Look for phrases such as "there is", "it was", "they were"
    • "It was determined by the class that the website was effective."
    • "The class determined that the website was effective."
    • 7 examples
  • Text should be perfect!
    • Online spell checkers
      • respelt.com
      • Tons more available
    • Grammar
      • Get a friend (or two or three) to proof read!

      • from explosm.net
    • Meaning and sentence structure
      • Take advantage of the University Writing Commons
    • Example from Amazon Prime: "whose" vs "who's"
    • Example from Lowe's: incorrect description
  • Sentence Variety
    • Subject First
      • The dog is usually well behaved. He barks a lot.
    • Single Word: However, first, hopefully
      • The dog is usually well behaved. However, he barks a lot.
    • Phrase
