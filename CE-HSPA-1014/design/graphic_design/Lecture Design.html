Designing with the Mind in Mind

"User-interface design rules and guidelines are more like laws than like rote recipes.

"Just as a set of laws is best applied and interpreted by lawyers and judges who are well versed in the laws, a set of user-interface design guidelines is best applied and interpreted by people who understand the basis for the guidelines and have learned from experience in applying them."

Jeff Johnson, Designing with the Mind in Mind, p. xii

"Technology—especially computer technology—advances quickly. The state of the art of computer-based interactive systems changes so quickly that it is difficult to get a book out before some of the technologies and designs mentioned in it are obsolete.

"On the other hand, the fundamentals of how people perceive, learn, and think do not change quickly. The basic operations of human perception and cognition have remained fairly stable for tens—perhaps even hundreds—of thousands of years."

Jeff Johnson, Designing with the Mind in Mind, p. 173

Four Basic Principles

"These principles are the underlying factors in every printed piece you see anywhere, on screen or anywhere else. If you just remember these four principles, your web (or printed) pages will look clean, neat, and professional. They will communicate more clearly, people will enjoy them more, and you will be proud."

Robin Williams, The Non-Designers Design Book, p. 105
  • Contrast
  • Repetition
  • Alignment
  • Proximity

These four basic principles are a distillation of the Gestalt principles of visual perception.

Contrast

"Contrast draws your eye into the page, it pulls you in."

Robin Williams, The Non-Designers Design Book, p. 115
  • Simply: Elements are visually different from each other.
  • The Rule: If two elements are not the same, make them very different.
  • Create a focal point and visual hierarchy. Lead.

In the example below, can you tell what text function as links? No, because there's no contrast between headers that are links and headers that are not links. The gray headers under "Top Local News" actually are links; the gray headers under "U.S./World News" are not.

Look at the last paragraph under "U.S./World News": it's in a different font than the rest of the text. That contrast would seem to indicate that it's somehow different than the rest of the paragraphs on the page...even though it's not.

Repetition

  • Simply: Certain elements are repeated in multiple places on your page/site.
  • The Rule: Repeat elements throughout your site to unify disparate parts.
  • Each page in the web site should look like it belongs to the same site.
  • Elements to repeat:
    • Colors
    • Style
    • Illustrations
    • Format
    • Layout
    • Typography
    • Etc.

Alignment

  • Simply: Items are lined up with each other.
  • The Rule: Choose one alignment (probably not center) and use it on the entire page.
  • It's all about the way your eyes move. Avoid the very left edge of the browser window... your eyes bump into it when they swing back to get the next line.

 

Careless writing or presentation of text can reduce skilled readers' automatic, context-free reading to conscious, context-based reading, burdening working memory, thereby decreasing speed and comprehension.

Jeff Johnson, Designing with the Mind in Mind, p. 39
  • Center alignment should almost never be used in websites.
  • It's good for wedding invitations and poetry, and that's about it.

Proximity

  • Simply: Items that are close together appear to have a relationship.
  • The Rule: Group items together that belong together.
  • Check with a squint test

 

Remember to take proximity into account when designing interaction. An example of poor proximity in interaction design was Google's Doodle winners gallery. Google must have realized this problem; their current design is much better.

Here's a screenshot from an AT&T FAQ page. Notice how the spacing around the headers isn't consistent, and how some headers are actually closer to the preceding paragraph than the following one.

 

And here's a screenshot from the Town of Henrietta's Residential Permit Applications page. Which link/form do the download buttons on the right correspond to?

Another example: The top row of Birst leadership team photos aren't next to the corresponding descriptions.

Grid Theory

It isn't just about making things line up, it's about proportion as well.

Golden Ratio

  • There is a "Golden Ratio", a mathematical pattern that occurs so often in nature that Pythagoras felt it was divinely inspired.
  • When things are designed with that ratio in mind, they are more pleasing visually.

Rule of Thirds

  • Divide page vertically and horizontally into thirds
  • Locating content along these lines and at the intersections makes designs more balanced
  • Users' eyes naturally follow the lines

  
Images from http://www.alibony.com/graphics/def_rule_of_thirds.html

 

In practice, many websites use the Golden Ratio and the Rule of Thirds as starting points only.


Image from http://desource.uvu.edu/dgm/2740/IN/steinja/lessons/03/l03_03.html

Balance

  • Symmetrical (formal) Balance is when elements of a composition are the same on either side of an axis line.
  • Horizontal (bilateral) symmetry (most common) can be applied by centering content or balancing it between columns.
  • Asymmetrical balance is also possible as long as the elements on both sides are weighted equally
  • Radial symmetry exists when elements are equally spaced around a central point.

Unity

  • The way in which the different elements of a page interact with one another.
  • A unified layout is one that works as a whole rather than being identified as separate pieces.
  • Uses the principles of Proximity and Repetition to achieve.

Color Schemes

  • The color scheme you choose says a lot about your site. Choose one that complements your site.
  • Paletton is a great reference.

Monochromatic

single base color and any number of tints or shades of that color

Analogous

colors that are adjacent to one another on the color wheel (don’t take a slice bigger than 1/3 of the whole)

Complementary 

colors that are directly opposite each other on the color wheel (don’t use in foreground/background combinations)

Split-complementary

use the two colors adjacent to your base color’s complement (can look juvenile and extreme)

Triadic

push the split-complements out one more notch on each side so that all colors are equally spaced around the color wheel

Tetradic

involves four colors, using one complementary color scheme with another complementary color scheme

Load Time (and Perceived Load Time!)

  • Think small whenever possible
  • "Visual appeal can be assessed within 50 ms"!
  • Resize/resample images
  • Include/link JavaScript at end of HTML page
  • Think about caching

Real Estate

  • Location, location, location!
  • Choose appropriate places for high-profile components such as logos, banners, page headings and navigation

Eye tracking

  • Eye tracking studies indicate eyes most often fixate first in upper left corner, hover then go left to right through rest of page.
  • Most “valuable” page real estate then is upper left, top center. Avoid placing important information and navigation on the far right side.
    Image from useit.com

Above the fold

  • After accounting for browser menus and controls, about 600 for 1024px x 768px resolutions
  • Amount of browser “chrome” (menus, address bar, scroll bar) varies by browser and user.

Horizontal Scrolling

  • Avoid!
  • Optimal width for 1024x768 is 960px.
  • Not all visitors will maximize viewport, however. Place less important stuff on the right, use % width of 100% or less, or fixed with 960px or less. Or if mobile users are target, much less.
  • Sometimes horizontal scrolling is ok, especially for artistic sites. For example:
    • TheHorizontalWay.com
    • Photo Gallery

 White Space

  • Use in areas around blocks of text (more readable), graphics (helps stand out), between blocks of text and images.
  • Actual amount depends on target audience.
  • Examples:
    • straightline.jp
    • After the terrorist attacks in Paris on November 13, 2015, Amazon reorganized their homepage to show just the French flag above the fold. The subsequent whitespace pushed the rest of the content down the page, leaving the user to focus on the flag.

Target Audience

Who you're designing a site for dramatically impacts what the site should look like.

Younger audiences

  • Children, pre-teens
  • Prefer bright, lively colors, lots of bright images and interactivity.
  • Ex: PBS Kids

Late teens, early twenties

  • Generally prefer dark background colors with occasional use of bright contrast, music and dynamic navigation.

"Everyone"

  • Neutral white background with splashes of color for interest and highlight page areas with black for text color to maximize readability.
  • Ex: Apple

Older

  • Light backgrounds, well-defined images and large text
  • Ex: AARP

Other Target Audience Considerations

  • Reading level
  • Animation
    • Only if it adds to your site
    • In general appeals to the younger audiences (kids).
    • Well-done navigation animation is ok/appealing
  • Browser
  • Color Blindness
    • Avoid using red, green, brown, grey or purple next to each other.
    • White, black and shades of blue and yellow are easier for these people to differentiate.

Images

  • Use only necessary images
  • Use thumbnails with links to larger images when appropriate
  • Use "alt" attribute to make site usable if images are not displayed
  • Use "title" attribute to increase usability and SEO
  • Resize/resample images to keep file size as small as possible
  • When resizing images, be sure to maintain aspect ratio!
